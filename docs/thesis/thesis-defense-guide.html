1. Research Overview

Simple Explanation: Imagine if your phone camera could look at a farm from space and tell you exactly what vitamins the soil is missing — that's essentially what we're building. Instead of expensive lab tests, we use free satellite photos + smart computer programs to diagnose soil health instantly.

This research develops a satellite-based soil nutrient assessment system using multispectral imagery from Sentinel-2 and Landsat-8 combined with ML (Machine Learning) to predict soil NPK — Nitrogen (N), Phosphorus (P), Potassium (K) — and pH in highland vegetable farms of the CAR (Cordillera Administrative Region), Philippines.

The Innovation

  • Input: Free satellite imagery + terrain data + local soil samples
  • Process: ML models — RF (Random Forest), XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting), SVR (Support Vector Regression) — trained on spectral-soil correlations
  • Output: 10m resolution soil fertility maps + crop-specific fertilizer recommendations
  • Delivery: Web-based decision support system for farmers and extension agents

4. Soil Science Fundamentals: NPK & pH

The Macronutrients Explained

Nitrogen (N)

Role: Chlorophyll production, protein synthesis, vegetative growth

Plant uptake forms: NO3- (nitrate) and NH4+ (ammonium)

Deficiency signs: Yellowing of older leaves, stunted growth

Excess effects: Excessive foliage, delayed maturity, disease susceptibility

Phosphorus (P)

Role: Energy transfer (ATP), root development, flowering/fruiting

Plant uptake form: H2PO4- and HPO42- (phosphate ions)

Deficiency signs: Purple/red leaves, poor root growth, delayed maturity

pH sensitivity: Most available at pH 6.0-7.0; locks up in acidic soils

Potassium (K)

Role: Water regulation, disease resistance, fruit quality, enzyme activation

Plant uptake form: K+ (potassium ion)

Deficiency signs: Leaf edge browning, weak stems, poor fruit quality

Mobility: Highly mobile in sandy soils; leaches with heavy rain

Soil pH and Nutrient Availability

Critical for CAR: Local studies show pH 4.49-5.2 (strongly acidic) due to continuous ammonium fertilizer use and heavy rainfall. At this pH, phosphorus becomes unavailable and aluminum toxicity occurs.
pH RangeClassificationNutrient Availability
< 4.5Extremely acidicAl/Mn toxicity; P, Ca, Mg severely limited
4.5 - 5.5Strongly acidicP fixation; reduced N, K availability
5.5 - 6.5Moderately acidicGood for most vegetables; optimal P availability
6.5 - 7.5NeutralMaximum nutrient availability range
> 7.5AlkalineFe, Mn, Zn, Cu deficiencies; P precipitation

Why Spectral Remote Sensing Can Detect Soil Nutrients

Soil nutrients affect spectral reflectance through multiple mechanisms:

  • Direct effects: Mineral composition affects SWIR reflectance; organic matter absorbs visible light
  • Indirect effects (vegetation): N status affects chlorophyll → changes red/NIR reflectance; K affects water content → SWIR changes
  • Soil color: Iron oxides, organic matter, moisture all create distinct spectral signatures

7. Spectral Indices Deep Dive

NDVI — Normalized Difference Vegetation Index

Measures plant health. Healthy plants absorb red light and reflect NIR (Near Infrared).

NDVI = (NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red) // Sentinel-2: (B8 - B4) / (B8 + B4) // Range: -1 to +1 // Healthy vegetation: 0.2 to 0.8 // Bare soil: ~0.1 // Water: negative values

Principle: Healthy plants absorb red light (chlorophyll) and strongly reflect NIR (cell structure). The ratio normalizes for illumination differences.

Limitation: Saturates at high biomass (>0.7); affected by soil background in sparse vegetation.

SAVI — Soil Adjusted Vegetation Index

Like NDVI but corrects for soil brightness. Better for sparse vegetation or early crop stages.

SAVI = ((NIR - Red) / (NIR + Red + L)) × (1 + L) // L = soil brightness correction factor // L = 0.5 for most conditions (moderate vegetation) // L = 1.0 for low vegetation cover // L = 0 reduces to NDVI

When to use: Sparse vegetation, early crop stages, arid regions. Critical for CAR highland farms with partial crop cover.

EVI — Enhanced Vegetation Index

Improved NDVI that reduces atmospheric noise. Best for dense vegetation areas.

EVI = 2.5 × ((NIR - Red) / (NIR + 6×Red - 7.5×Blue + 1)) // Reduces atmospheric and canopy background noise // Better for high-biomass areas

BSI — Bare Soil Index

Detects exposed soil without vegetation. Higher values = more bare soil visible.

BSI = ((SWIR1 + Red) - (NIR + Blue)) / ((SWIR1 + Red) + (NIR + Blue)) // Higher values = more exposed soil // Essential for identifying bare soil areas for direct soil analysis

NDRE — Normalized Difference Red Edge

Uses the "red edge" band (705nm) which is super sensitive to nitrogen status in plants.

NDRE = (NIR - RedEdge) / (NIR + RedEdge) // Sentinel-2: (B8 - B5) / (B8 + B5) // More sensitive to chlorophyll variations than NDVI // Better for detecting N (Nitrogen) stress in crops

Key advantage: Less saturation than NDVI in dense canopies; more sensitive to subtle N deficiency.

SSRI — Standardized Spectral Reflectance Index

Novel index created using PCA (Principal Component Analysis) to combine multiple bands optimally.

SSRI = Derived from Principal Component Analysis (PCA) of Sentinel-2 bands // Novel index from Li et al. (2025) // Achieved R² > 0.68 for nitrogen prediction // Reduces multicollinearity between correlated bands

8. Machine Learning Models

Simple Analogy: Teaching ML to predict soil nutrients is like teaching someone to identify ripe mangoes. You show them 300 mangoes with lab-confirmed ripeness levels. They learn patterns: "yellow color + sweet smell + slight softness = ripe." Now they can predict ripeness of NEW mangoes without cutting them open. We do the same — show the computer 300+ satellite images with lab-confirmed soil nutrients, it learns the patterns, then predicts nutrients for any farm.

Random Forest (RF)

Type: Ensemble of decision trees

How it works: Builds many trees on random subsets of data/features; averages predictions

Strengths:

  • Handles 50+ features well
  • Built-in feature importance
  • Robust to outliers
  • Resistant to overfitting

Best for: High-dimensional spectral data; provides interpretable importance rankings

XGBoost

Type: Gradient boosted trees

How it works: Sequentially builds trees, each correcting errors of previous

Strengths:

  • Often highest accuracy
  • Fast training (GPU support)
  • Handles missing values
  • Regularization built-in

Best for: When maximum prediction accuracy is priority

SVR (Support Vector Regression)

Type: Kernel-based regression

How it works: Maps data to high-dimensional space; finds optimal hyperplane

Strengths:

  • Works well with smaller datasets
  • Effective in high dimensions
  • Memory efficient
  • Flexible kernels (RBF, linear)

Best for: When sample size is limited; soil pH prediction

9. Spatial Cross-Validation Explained

The Problem: Spatial Autocorrelation

What is it? Nearby soil samples tend to have similar values ("everything is related to everything else, but near things are more related" - Tobler's First Law of Geography).

Why it matters: Standard K-fold CV randomly splits data, so training and test sets contain nearby points. The model appears accurate but is partially "memorizing" local patterns rather than learning generalizable relationships.

The Solution: Spatial CV

Ensure geographic separation between training and test sets:

  • Leave-one-site-out: Train on 4 municipalities, test on the 5th
  • Spatial blocking: Divide study area into geographic blocks; each block serves as test set once
  • Buffer exclusion: Exclude points within X meters of test points from training

This Study's Approach

5-fold spatial cross-validation:

  • Data stratified by municipality AND elevation zone
  • Each fold represents distinct geographic area
  • Model trains on 4 folds, validates on 1
  • Final metrics = average across all 5 folds

Hybrid Approaches in Literature

Recent research shows combining ML with geostatistics improves accuracy:

RFOK (Random Forest + Ordinary Kriging): 1. RF predicts soil property based on covariates 2. Calculate prediction residuals (actual - predicted) 3. Interpolate residuals using kriging 4. Final prediction = RF prediction + kriged residual // Result: RMSE decreased by 62.2% vs RF alone (2025 study)

Processing Steps

  1. Band harmonization: Resample to 30m common grid (cubic convolution — a smoothing method for resizing images)
  2. Temporal matching: Satellite scenes ±5 days of soil sampling
  3. Spatial matching: GPS coordinates to pixel using ±10m buffer; extract mean reflectance
  4. Cloud masking: Remove cloud-affected pixels using SCL (Scene Classification Layer) band
  5. Outlier removal: IQR (Interquartile Range) method — removes extreme values that are >1.5× the middle 50% spread
  6. Normalization: Min-max scaling to [0,1] range
  7. Missing imputation: KNN (K-Nearest Neighbors) with K=5 — fills missing values using the average of 5 most similar data points

Evaluation Metrics — What They Actually Mean

R² (R-squared) — "How much does your model explain?"

If soil nitrogen varies across 100 farms, R² tells you what percentage of that variation your model correctly predicts.

  • R² = 0.00 — Model explains nothing (just guessing randomly)
  • R² = 0.50 — Model explains 50% of variation (okay)
  • R² = 0.65 — Model explains 65% of variation (our target)
  • R² = 0.80 — Model explains 80% of variation (excellent)
  • R² = 1.00 — Perfect prediction (never happens in real data)

Think of it as a grade: 0.65 = 65% = passing. Higher is better.

R² = 1 - (SS_residual / SS_total) // SS_residual = sum of squared errors (how wrong we were) // SS_total = total variance in data (how much values vary) // Result: 0 to 1 scale; higher = better RMSE (Root Mean Square Error) = √(Σ(predicted - actual)² / n) // Average size of prediction errors in original units (e.g., kg/ha for nitrogen) // Lower = better. RMSE = 5 means "we're typically off by about 5 units" MAE (Mean Absolute Error) = Σ|predicted - actual| / n // Similar to RMSE but less affected by outliers (extreme errors) // Lower = better

SHAP for Interpretability

SHapley Additive exPlanations: Explains which features contributed most to each prediction.

  • Validates model reasoning aligns with domain knowledge
  • Builds trust with end-users
  • Identifies unexpected feature importance (potential data issues)

13. Fertilizer Recommendation Algorithm

Input: Predicted N, P, K, pH from best ML model + Crop type + Previous crop + Target yield Step 1: Compare predicted vs optimal nutrient levels for crop Step 2: Calculate deficiency = optimal - predicted Step 3: Apply pH correction factors (availability adjustment) Step 4: Apply rotation credits (e.g., -20% N after legumes) Step 5: Convert nutrient needs to fertilizer rates Output: kg/ha of specific fertilizer products + lime if needed

14. Validation Strategy

Model Validation

  • 20% independent holdout test set
  • Metrics: R², RMSE, MAE
  • Leave-one-site-out validation
  • Temporal stability (dry vs wet season)

Field Validation

  • RCBD (Randomized Complete Block Design) — standard experimental layout that controls for field variability
  • 3 crops × 3 elevations × 2 seasons
  • Compare: Farmer practice vs STK (Soil Test Kit — traditional lab method) vs Satellite prediction

18. Defense Q&A: General Questions

Q: Explain your research in simple terms that anyone can understand.
Answer:

"We use satellite images to check soil health without expensive lab tests. The satellite captures light reflected from the earth - different soils reflect light differently based on their nutrient content. We teach a computer to recognize these patterns using actual soil samples, then the computer can predict nutrients for any farm location. Finally, we tell farmers exactly how much fertilizer to apply based on what their soil needs."

Q: Why is this research important? Why should anyone care?
Answer:
  • Food security: 80% of PH farmlands have low fertility; we help optimize production
  • Farmer economics: Reduces fertilizer waste by 15-25%, saving thousands of pesos per hectare
  • Environment: Prevents over-fertilization that pollutes water and releases greenhouse gases
  • Accessibility: Free satellite data makes precision agriculture available to smallholders, not just large farms
  • CAR specifically: "Salad Bowl of the Philippines" - 432,000 MT vegetables annually
Q: Most soil remote sensing studies use European data (LUCAS). Will that actually work here in the Philippines / Southeast Asia?
This is exactly why we're collecting LOCAL soil samples.

The short answer: European MODELS won't work directly — but the European METHODOLOGY does. That's the key distinction.

What transfers (universal physics):
  • Spectral physics is universal — SWIR absorption by clay minerals works the same way whether you're in Germany or Benguet
  • The relationship patterns — "more organic matter = darker soil = lower reflectance" is true everywhere
  • The ML workflow — collect samples → extract spectral features → train model → validate
  • Sentinel-2 bands — the satellite sees Philippine soil the same way it sees European soil
What does NOT transfer (requires local calibration):
  • Model coefficients — A model trained on German soil will predict WRONG values for CAR soil
  • Nutrient ranges — European "low nitrogen" might be "medium" in Philippine volcanic soils
  • Soil types — CAR has Andisols (volcanic); Europe has mostly Alfisols/Mollisols
  • Climate effects — Tropical weathering creates different mineral compositions
Our solution — LOCAL CALIBRATION:
  • We collect 300+ soil samples from CAR itself
  • We train models on Philippine soil + Philippine satellite data
  • European studies prove the METHOD works; our samples make it work HERE
Think of it like a recipe: European studies proved "satellite + ML = soil prediction" works. But we can't use their exact recipe — we need to adjust the ingredients for local conditions. That's what our 300+ samples do.

19. Defense Q&A: Technical Questions

Q: Why Sentinel-2 specifically? Why not other satellites?
  • Free and open: No licensing costs through Copernicus program
  • High resolution: 10m bands vs 30m for Landsat
  • Red-edge bands: Unique 705-783nm bands sensitive to N status - Landsat lacks these
  • Frequent revisit: 5 days = more cloud-free opportunities in CAR's cloudy climate
  • Level-2A: Pre-corrected atmospheric effects saves processing time
Q: Why use machine learning instead of simple linear regression?
  • Non-linear relationships: Soil-spectral correlations are complex, not straight lines
  • High dimensionality: 50+ features; linear regression struggles with multicollinearity
  • Interactions: ML captures feature interactions (e.g., vegetation × terrain effects)
  • Noise tolerance: Ensemble methods robust to outliers common in RS data
  • Literature consensus: ML consistently outperforms linear methods for soil prediction
Q: Explain spatial cross-validation. Why not regular K-fold?

Problem: Nearby soil samples are similar (spatial autocorrelation). Regular K-fold randomly mixes nearby points into training and test sets, making the model appear more accurate than it really is.

Solution: Spatial CV ensures geographic separation. Example: train on La Trinidad + Atok + Kibungan + Bauko, test on Tinoc. The model must generalize to a completely new area.

Result: More realistic accuracy estimates that reflect real-world performance on new farms.

Q: What is SHAP and why do you use it?

SHAP = SHapley Additive exPlanations - a method to explain ML predictions.

For each prediction, SHAP shows how much each feature contributed. If SHAP says SWIR bands are most important for pH prediction, we can verify this matches soil science (SWIR is sensitive to mineralogy). It builds trust with users who want to understand "why" not just "what."

Q: Why is R² ≥ 0.65 your target? Is that good enough?
  • Literature benchmark: Zhang et al. achieved 0.74-0.84 in optimal conditions; 0.65+ is reasonable for complex tropical highlands
  • Practical sufficiency: R²=0.65 means we explain 65% of nutrient variation - enough to distinguish high vs low fertility zones and guide appropriate fertilizer rates
  • Not a replacement: We complement, not replace, lab testing for critical decisions
  • Exceeding target: With local calibration + terrain data, we may achieve 0.70+

20. Defense Q&A: Methodology Questions

Q: How did you determine your sample size of 300+?
  • Rule of thumb: 10 samples per feature → 50 features × 10 = 500 ideal; 300 is practical compromise
  • Stratification: 5 municipalities × 3 elevations × 3 slopes × 7 crops = many strata to cover
  • Literature precedent: Similar studies used 200-500 samples successfully
  • Resource constraints: Lab costs, field accessibility, project timeline
Q: Why January-March for data collection?
  • Dry season: Fewer clouds = more usable satellite images
  • Pre-planting: More bare soil visible for direct soil spectral analysis
  • Stable conditions: Soil moisture more consistent than monsoon season
  • Temporal matching: ±5 days between sampling and satellite pass minimizes change
Q: How do you handle vegetation covering the soil?
  • Timing: Sample during pre-planting or early growth stages
  • Indirect sensing: Vegetation health reflects soil N status (via NDVI, NDRE)
  • Spectral indices: SAVI and BSI designed to account for vegetation effects
  • SWIR penetration: Shortwave infrared partially penetrates thin canopies
  • Acknowledged limitation: Full crop cover reduces accuracy; future work could add radar (SAR)
Q: What if model performs well for N but poorly for P?

This is expected and handled:

  • Different nutrients have different spectral signatures
  • N affects vegetation (easy to detect); P has weaker direct signal
  • We train separate models per nutrient - best algorithm may differ
  • Report confidence levels with predictions
  • Recommend lab testing for nutrients with lower model confidence

21. Defense Q&A: ML-Specific Questions

Q: How does Random Forest work?
  1. Create many decision trees (e.g., 500)
  2. Each tree trained on random subset of samples (bootstrap)
  3. At each split, consider random subset of features
  4. Final prediction = average of all tree predictions

Why it works: Individual trees may overfit, but averaging many trees cancels out errors. Random feature selection ensures trees are diverse.

Q: What's the difference between Random Forest and XGBoost?
AspectRandom ForestXGBoost

Q: What is Bayesian optimization for hyperparameter tuning?

Instead of trying all combinations (grid search) or random sampling:

  1. Build probabilistic model of objective function
  2. Use model to predict which hyperparameters might be best
  3. Try those hyperparameters, update model with result
  4. Repeat - focuses search on promising regions

Advantage: Finds good hyperparameters in fewer iterations (100 vs 1000s for grid search).

Q: How do you prevent overfitting?
  • Spatial cross-validation: Tests generalization to new areas
  • Regularization: XGBoost has L1/L2 penalties; RF limits tree depth
  • Early stopping: Stop training when validation error increases
  • Feature selection: Remove redundant features that add noise
  • Holdout test set: Final evaluation on never-seen data

Full Definitions (Plain English)

TermWhat It Actually Means
MultispectralA camera that sees more than human eyes — captures invisible light (infrared, etc.) in addition to regular colors. Like having X-ray vision for plants and soil.
Spectral reflectanceHow much light bounces back from a surface. Different soils/plants reflect light differently — that's how satellites "see" what's on the ground.
Level-2APre-cleaned satellite data. ESA already removed clouds and atmospheric haze — ready to use out of the box.
NDVINormalized Difference Vegetation Index — A number from -1 to +1 showing plant health. Green healthy plants = high NDVI (~0.6-0.8). Dead/bare soil = low NDVI (~0.1).
SAVISoil Adjusted Vegetation Index — Like NDVI but smarter. Works better when you can see soil between plants (sparse crops, early growth).
SWIRShortwave Infrared — Invisible light (1500-2500nm wavelength). Soil minerals and moisture absorb SWIR differently — this is our SECRET WEAPON for predicting NPK and pH.
Red EdgeA special zone (705-783nm) where plant leaves change from absorbing to reflecting light. Super sensitive to nitrogen levels in plants.
DEMDigital Elevation Model — A height map showing terrain. Tells us if a farm is on a ridge, valley, or slope. Elevation affects soil nutrients.
TPITopographic Position Index — Is this spot higher or lower than its surroundings? Ridges drain nutrients; valleys collect them.
Random ForestAn ML algorithm that builds hundreds of decision trees (like flowcharts) and averages their answers. "Wisdom of the crowd" for computers.
XGBoostExtreme Gradient Boosting — An ML algorithm that builds trees one-by-one, each fixing mistakes of the previous. Often the most accurate.
SVRSupport Vector Regression — An ML algorithm that finds the best line/curve through data points. Works well with smaller datasets.
Spatial autocorrelationNearby things are similar. Soil in Farm A is probably similar to soil in neighboring Farm B. We must account for this or our accuracy looks artificially high.
SHAPSHapley Additive exPlanations — A method to explain WHY the computer made a prediction. "SWIR band contributed 40% to this pH prediction."
R²R-squared — How much variation our model explains. R²=0.70 means we explain 70% of the pattern. Higher = better. 0.65+ is our target.
RMSERoot Mean Square Error — Average size of our prediction mistakes. RMSE = 5 for nitrogen means we're typically off by about 5 units.
SSNMSite-Specific Nutrient Management — Giving each field exactly what IT needs, not a one-size-fits-all fertilizer rate.
TAMTechnology Acceptance Model — A survey framework asking: "Do users find this useful? Easy to use? Will they actually adopt it?"
SARAiSmarter Approaches to Reinvigorate Agriculture as an Industry — DOST's program using satellites + ML for Philippine agriculture. We align with this.
Kjeldahl methodThe standard lab test for nitrogen. Acid digestion converts all nitrogen to ammonia, which is measured. Named after the Danish chemist who invented it.
Olsen methodThe standard lab test for available phosphorus. Uses sodium bicarbonate to extract P from soil.
